# Prose Control

Use these controls to make the execution brief useful for prose generation without writing the prose itself.

## Core Principle

Write constraints for the future scene, not the scene.

## Show Through Pressure

- Do not state a character trait directly when an action, refusal, delay, mistake, or choice can reveal it.
- Do not explain a relationship directly when a boundary, habit, avoidance, or choice can show it.
- Do not summarize the plot. Keep the brief focused on what the prose must stage in the present scene.

## Setting Exposure

- Do not explain setting mechanics as exposition.
- Present setting through obstacle, cost, procedural friction, missing information, wrong assumption, physical trace, or consequence.
- If the character does not understand the mechanism, describe only what they can notice or infer.

## Dialogue Control

- Give dialogue function, topic, pressure, and subtext.
- Do not write full dialogue lines unless the author explicitly asks for sample lines.
- Specify what each speaker cannot say because of knowledge, motive, status, or information-hiding rules.
- Set a rough line win-rate for dominant characters. If one character has already landed force, shift the next beat to action, interruption, silence, failed repair, or another character's pressure.

## Viewpoint Control

- Lock the viewpoint character.
- Keep perception, inference, and emotional naming within that character's current knowledge.
- If readers may know more than the viewpoint character, mark it as reader tension, surface clue, or dramatic irony, not as viewpoint knowledge.
- Track the key support character's knowledge and emotion separately when their reaction drives the scene.

## Pressure and Misread Control

- Identify one protected pressure point for the scene: action, object, money/procedure, face, relationship, or information boundary.
- Keep one dominant joke, misread, or comic pressure. Treat extra cleverness as optional.
- Do not replace a summary joke with prettier narration; convert it into action, object, procedure mismatch, relationship reaction, or broken dialogue.
- Keep blunt or low-grade misreads tactful and double-coded through hesitation, official wording, avoided questions, or formal procedure.

## Rhythm Control

- Set approximate dialogue ratio when useful: low, medium, high.
- Set psychological density: sparse, moderate, dense.
- Set paragraph rhythm: short pressure beats, mixed action/dialogue, or longer observational blocks.
- Keep instructions functional, not literary.

## Forbidden Drift

Prevent these common failures:

- Explaining what the scene means instead of staging what happens.
- Making the protagonist know hidden information.
- Turning a brief into polished prose.
- Adding new events to make the scene more dramatic.
- Repeating chapter-level summary instead of specifying scene-level execution.
- Letting a high-risk scene skip the one turn that changes face, money/procedure, object control, relationship pressure, or information boundary.
